MONTESSORI CONFERENCE

We enjoyed our informative and educational weekend in Sarasota. Thank you to Keri Matthews and Teri Aboulafia for their help with the children last Thursday. This allowed our LE teachers to leave school in time to attend the Thursday reception at the conference. This is a small conference with meals served communally giving everyone time to connect with old friends and make new ones. Attendees came from as far away as Japan and South Africa.

 

Over the course of the weekend we were able to attend a variety of workshops on Montessori philosophy and practice. We also heard several keynote speakers. Dr. Andrew Cutler, psychiatric physician, researcher, Montessori parent, and ADHD adult, shared current understanding of brain development and neurobiology of ADHD and other related brain conditions. He explained that Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder is a complete misnomer. There is no attention deficit but rather a problem with attention regulation. Hyperactivity is not always present, and it is a brain condition not a disorder. It is found in 10% of the population world- wide and is highly genetic. Incidence of ADHD is three to four times higher among police, fireman, professional athletes, and chefs. Children diagnosed with ADHD often lag behind their peers emotionally by as much as two years. The ADHD brain uses nonlinear processing, has a variable sense of time, is reactive rather than proactive, seeks immediate gratification, lives intensely in the moment, and is easily bored.

 

Stephanie Miller, executive director of the Trust for Learning urged us to work toward a collaborative Montessori movement to make the approach accessible to significantly more children across the United States. She shared the bold vision of a group of Montessori leaders. In this vision the Federal Department of Education works closely with a group of unified and effective Montessori leadership and that the Montessori method has proven to be an invaluable educational approach worthy of focused funding streams. This vision went on to imagine that a new culture of life-long learning and self-reliance flourishes and the centuries-old factory model of education finally closes its doors. The Trust for Learning is a collaborative fund focusing on three pillars of high quality education: start a child's education in the earliest years, build social-emotional skills along with academic skills, and place the child at the center of the educational design.

 

She told us about a new project designed to bring awareness of Montessori education to the mainstream, A feature length film being made to "shine a light on Montessori education". Building the Pink Tower asks the question, what is the true goal of education? Go to  www.buildingthepinktower.org to see a trailer of the film.
