EXECUTIVE FUNCTIONS  

Over 100 years ago, Maria Montessori envisioned schools that went beyond the mere transmission of skills and knowledge.  She wanted to educate children to make good choices, take on challenges, make connections, think critically, and to become self-directed engaged learners.  As usual, she was well ahead of her time.  Today there is a lot of buzz about executive functions.  These functions involve the prefrontal cortex of the brain and involve combining social, emotional, and intellectual capacities.  They emerge during the preschool years and mature in young adulthood.  In her book, Mind in the Making, Ellen Galinsky identifies seven essential life skills associated with the development of executive functioning.  Our staff is reading and discussing this book together.  As we go through it, I will occasionally share some of the concepts we are learning.

 

One of the skills discussed is focus and self control.  The Montessori learning environment encourages students to focus and the freedoms given them require them to exercise self control.  Children must practice inhibitory control by learning to stop and think before they act and to stick with an activity even after an initial failure.  Controlling attention, emotions, and behavior to achieve a goal are all involved.  You can help with the development of these skills at home.  Play games that promote focus by requiring kids to pay attention like guessing games, puzzles, red light/green light, or Simon says, which also promotes working memory.  Turn off the television when no one is watching. This background television  has been shown to  affect the quality of children's play and their focus.  Finally, pay attention to the content of television shows, video games, or movies your child watches.  Studies tell us that aggression begets aggression as children imitate aggressive acts they've seen.  Research has also shown that children viewing this type of media learn ways of thinking that lead to violence like retaliation and categorizing people as good guys or bad guys.  It is important for you to take a strong role in selecting media that will not hinder your child's capacity to focus and exercise self control.
