HOW CHILDREN SUCCEED  

In the current issue of Montessori Life, professional journal of the American Montessori Society, Paul Tough's book How Children Succeed is reviewed.  The book focuses on the increasing knowledge base supporting non-cognitive skill development in children.  Tough maintains that the qualities that matter most for success in life have more to do with character traits, perseverance, curiosity, self control, emotional regulation, and other executive function skills.  Although Tough has not looked at Montessori education, his book is one more 21st century validation of Dr. Montessori's pioneering work.  As I said last week, the Montessori learning environment encourages the development of executive functions, as children choose work, prioritize activities, practice time management, learn to participate appropriately in discussions, reflect on their successes, and help a friend.

UPPER ELEMENTARY NEWS

We had fun following the Winter Olympics and creating graphs to track the medal counts.  In addition to the many trade goods that traveled the Silk Road, students learned that there was a great exchange of ideas.  Representatives of all the world's great religions traveled along the route.  Students learned about the Golden Rule of Reciprocity, a universal moral code found in many religions.  Each student created a small banner with their own golden rule and an illustration.  Math work with factor trees and powers of numbers is ongoing, along with the parts of the circle and finding circumference.  Practice with transition words and Latin roots and prefixes continue.  To follow up work with physical and chemical changes, we will try our hands at creating polymers later this week.
