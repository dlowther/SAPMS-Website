BEING A MONTESSORI PARENT

You took the first step to becoming a Montessori parent when you enrolled your child in a Montessori school.  This is a big one, as most of you did not attend Montessori school.  This may be a bit out of your comfort zone, as the school does not offer the familiar benchmarks of success, grades and test scores. But remember that Montessori schools have educated generations of children all around the world for over 100 years.  Montessori's methods and insights were ahead of her time; but continue to be validated by today's developmental and educational psychology. To make the most of your decision, here are some ways that you can reinforce your child's school experience by using Montessori principles at home.

 

Encourage Independence:  Always allow your children to do things for themselves.  Can they make their own breakfast, pack their own lunch, or make their own bed, pick up their own clothes, set the table, or put dishes in the dishwasher?  Give your children chores they can accomplish and allow them time to do so.  This builds responsibility, which in turn builds independence, which builds confidence.  Offer choices whenever possible. Would you like cereal or eggs for breakfast?

 

Establish Order:  Montessori classrooms are known for their beauty and order.  Establishing order at home helps children become more independent.  Start with the child's bedroom by providing shelves for their toys and belongings.  Make sure even the youngest child can reach hooks and rods to hang up their own clothes.  Older children will appreciate having a desk and organization products like CD holders.  Reduce clutter by rotating some toys into storage instead of having everything out at once.

 

Develop Concentration:   Encourage your child's focus and concentration by paying attention to what sparks enthusiasm and focus, and then make sure you provide opportunities and materials to facilitate this.  When your child is engaged and focused on something, do not interrupt her until she is finished.  Limit exposure to TV and video. TV's constantly changing images may keep your child quietly hypnotized for long periods of time, but this is not concentration.

 

Use Encouragement, Not Rewards or Treats:  If a task is worth doing, it has value for its own sake, and encouragement is enough. Encourage with comments like, "You worked really hard on that."  Rewards will have a short term effect, but sends the message that the only reason to do the task is for the reward.  Encouragement focuses on the amount of effort, persistence, determination, and joy.
UPPER ELEMENTARY NEWS  

We had a great time celebrating Pi Day (3.14) the day before spring break.  Pi necklaces and a pi chain were made using the color coding for the Montessori bead bars (1-9).  We heard a pi rap, sang pi carols, and of course, ate pie! Everyone had to earn their piece by computing the circumference of the pie. Students got a head start on National Poetry Month, April, with a mini poetry workshop on the work of American poet William Carlos Williams.  They were intrigued with his work, which bears little resemblance to their idea of poetry. Everyone wrote several poems using his style.
