EXTRINSIC REWARDS AND MONTESSORI EDUCATION

Those of you who are new to Montessori education, may have noticed that there are never stars, stickers, smiley faces, or grades on your child's work.  This does not mean that teachers are not evaluating children's work.  Rather teacher evaluation is usually invisible to the children or may take the form of matter of fact notes on their papers (check capitalization and punctuation).  Dr. Montessori observed that rewards and evaluations interfered with children's learning. Elementary age children in Montessori classrooms become readily engaged in their lessons and activities.  They come to view learning as an enjoyable activity to engage in with concentration.  Dr. Montessori saw that both adult praise and correction tended to disrupt self- guiding concentration.  Many of our activities provide opportunities for children to correct their own work.  While the use of extrinsic motivators may have a positive effect in the short run, research has shown that tying rewards to an activity negatively impacts motivation to engage in the desired behavior once the reward is withdrawn.

UPPER ELEMENTARY NEWS
Upper Elementary students started their week with a trip to the GTM Reserve, where they learned about marine debris and its effect all along the food chain.  

Older students have been working on idioms, singular and plural possessives, and learning to write a thesis statement.  In math, mean, mode, range, and median, order of operations, adding fractions with different denominators, Hands on Equations, are being covered.  Younger students are reviewing nouns and logical adjective/noun pairs in language arts and types of triangles, test tube division in math.

 

We would like every student to bring an old pair of shoes that can remain at school.  These will be their outside shoes to wear for PE and other outdoor field activities.  This will help keep our classroom clean.
