MONTESSORI MATH

In the Montessori classroom, math education is focused on the process not the answer.  We focus on how the child gets the answer, on promoting understanding, and learning how to think with precision and logic.  Dr. Montessori called this the development of the "mathematical mind".  Rules become the point of arrival not the point of departure.  The Montessori materials: incorporate consistent shapes and colors, introduce one concept at a time within the context of what is already known, and are sequenced so the child progresses from the concrete to the abstract. The materials are not a crutch, instead they are an aid to understanding.  Once the children understand a concept, they put the material aside.


The materials are multi-sensory, hands-on, and their use involves movement.  They promote active rather than passive learning by asking the children to discover concepts for themselves.  This is why we ask parents to refrain from teaching your child "tricks" or "shortcuts" that you have used in math problem solving.  These remove the child's incentive to continue with the materials to understand where the answer comes from and what it means.  We don't want to trade quick right answers for deeper understanding, long term learning, and the building of your child's brain.

UPPER ELEMENTARY NEWS

Students are building atomic models of various chemical elements using the Bohr Atomic Diagram Board after learning about the parts of the atom. In geography we are looking at climate zones, exploring latitude and longitude,  looking at biomes of the world, and testing our knowledge of Africa with a quiz.  The study of perimeter, area of triangles, and trapezoids, and types of triangles are our recent geometry work.  Children are also working with fractions and decimals, long multiplication, long division, the algebraic Snake Game, and evaluating algebraic expressions.  Language arts work includes lessons on supporting topic sentences in composition, using prepositions and prepositional phrases along with daily work in our new spelling program, "Spellwell".  Our literature group is discussing a humorous short story, "Piers and the Begonia".

"There is abundant research showing that movement and cognition are closely intertwined. People represent spaces and objects more accurately, remember information better, and show superior social cognition when their movements are aligned with what they are thinking about or learning.  Traditional classrooms are not set up to capitalize on the relationship between movement and cognition.  In contrast, Montessori has movement at its core."    

 

-- Angeline Lillard, Montessori Educator and Developmental Psychologist
