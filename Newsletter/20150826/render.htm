Cosmic Education

Cosmic Education, the philosophical foundation of the Montessori elementary curriculum, provides the conceptual framework for the study of history, biology, physical science, chemistry and geography.  Cosmic Education was Maria Montessori's hope for a peaceful future. She felt that by showing that the universe is a web of relationships with everything interconnected, we would raise generations of children committed to peace. Cosmic Education is delivered to the students in a series of "Great Lessons".  The main ones are the Story of the Creation of the Universe, the Story of Life on Earth, the Story of Humans and the Story of Math and the Story of Language.  The children are presented with the big picture as a context for their studies which allows them to unify knowledge. These big lessons are presented in story form giving the learner an emotional tie that makes the knowledge more meaningful. This guides the children into further study by stimulating their imagination and interest.

In preparation for the scientific creation story, the Big Bang, teachers share creation stories from different cultures around the world. This is to introduce the child to the many ways people have imagined the world coming into existence. The idea is to introduce the child to different cultural perspectives of different people on Earth. Early in the school year, elementary classrooms share these creation stories with the children.  They tell the children that people have always wondered about how the world began.  Many of the stories were passed down from parents to their children long before writing was invented.  They were retold from generation to generation and sometimes storytellers would add details from their own imaginations or leave out other details. Finally, the story which had passed through the imaginations of many people was written down.  After hearing and discussing a number of stories, children will have the opportunity to write their own creation story drawing on what they heard and their own families' beliefs.
School Safety
We have instituted a new visitor sign-in protocol to improve security.  This is a little different from the system used in the past.  The sign-in is on the shelf right inside the front door.  Complete both the badge and sign out stub by listing your name, your destination, the date, your initials, and time of arrival.  Apply the badge to your clothing in a visible place. Before you leave the building, return to the office to sign yourself out.  List your departure time on the stub and remove your badge.  Conscientious observation of these procedures will better enable the school to ensure the safety of all building occupants, including students, employees, and visitors.

We have also begun to keep the front door locked.  Please ring the doorbell (new), and someone will admit you.  When entering and leaving, please be mindful to pull the door completely shut.  It does not close completely automatically.  You must pull it until it latches.

Thank you for your cooperation and support with these new procedures.

Upper Elementary
Here at the Galimore Center we've spent the last couple of weeks forming and reinforcing our Montessori classroom community. Our primary focus has been routine-establishment, goal- setting for the new year, and formulating our rights and regulations within our classroom community.

The class collaboratively researched and wrote our own Classroom Bill of
Rights based on the United Nations Universal Bill of Children's Rights. The students also wrote letters to their future selves, which have been enclosed in Time Capsules decorated with collaborative artwork, to be buried for the year at the main campus upon our arrival. These letters will provide a glimpse into each students' life at the start of a new portion of the Montessori cycle; they include personal, social, and academic goals for the year.

We also have established a forum for children to express their opinions and concerns about their direct environment at a weekly Community Meeting; a meeting led, recorded, and collectively guided by the students themselves. We have been researching and modeling an egalitarian, collaborative decision- making process which is peaceful and provides student- generated solutions to issues in our classroom.

In science, the children have been engrossed in the scientific method and in geography with biome studies of North America, specifically wetlands and estuaries. Cultural studies have begun with an in- depth look at the Timucua culture, which has inspired many interpretative works. The students continue to work productively in math both abstractlly and with concrete materials.Study of the functions of words in grammar continues as well as many other aspects of linguistic endeavors.

The Galimore Center has provided a great opportunity to explore nature, swim regularly, and enjoy kickball Fridays. We have been pleased and impressed with the classes' ability to adapt, work diligently and come together as a peaceful and productive Montessori community.
