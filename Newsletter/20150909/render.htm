The Science Behind the Genius

Over 100 years ago, Dr. Maria Montessori devised a method for educating children based on her observations of how children actually learn.  In her book, The Science Behind the Genius, Dr. Angeline Lillard, a developmental psychologist and second generation Montessori educator, shows how current scientific research supports Montessori's major insights.  In this book Lillard presents research concerning each of eight insights that form the foundation of Montessori education.

The first Montessori principle discussed is that movement and cognition are closely entwined. Montessori observed that in young children thinking seemed to be expressed by the hands before it can be put into words.  She said that in small children, thinking and moving are the same process.  She continued to see this correlation in the elementary age child.  Our Montessori learning environments are rich in opportunities for movement and object manipulation.  Many of our materials are designed to expose the child's hand to abstract concepts.  This is especially true in the area of mathematics but also present in other areas of the curriculum.  Children use puzzle maps and pin maps to learn about geography.  Movement is incorporated in reading and language arts as well.  Young children may walk around the room placing cards next to the object they name. This is teaching the concept of nouns.  Command cards are used to teach other parts of speech.  They range from simple one word commands such as "walk" or "jump" to teach verbs to more complex commands like, "tiptoe rapidly to the door, then tiptoe slowly back to your seat".  This is teaching the concept of adverbs.

Children in Montessori classrooms move freely about the room working at tables or on the floor.  They carry materials around the room.  As they manipulate materials they are learning basic concepts in all subject areas.  This is in sharp contrast to a traditional classroom where students sit in desks all day with minimal opportunity for movement.  Dr. Montessori had profound respect for the integration of movement and cognition and advised that children be given incentives and opportunities to move their bodies in purposeful ways.  Dr. Lillard's examination of current research revealed that findings imply that education should involve movement to enhance learning.

This book will be available for parents to borrow in our new lending library which will be open soon.
